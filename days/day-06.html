Caching
The Librarian Who Reads Your Mind

The single highest-leverage trick in all of system design: stop asking the slow thing the same question twice. Today you learn exactly where to put the cache, what to do when it's wrong, and why "just add a cache" is both the best and most dangerous advice in the field.

The Story

Meet Cache Cathy, who sits at a small desk right by the front door. Behind her, down a long hallway, is Database Dave — reliable, complete, but slow to walk to.

When a visitor asks for something, Cathy checks her desk first. If it's there (a cache hit), she hands it over instantly. If it's not (a cache miss), she jogs down the hallway, asks Dave, brings the answer back — and, this is the important part, leaves a copy on her desk for the next person who asks the same thing. Cathy's desk is small, though, so when it fills up she throws out whatever nobody's asked for in a while to make room (eviction).

What should stay in your head
Cache = Frequently used information stored somewhere much closer and faster than its original, permanent home.

Caching Patterns

PatternHow it worksTradeoff
Cache-asideApp checks cache first; on miss, reads DB and writes result into cache itselfSimple, most common. Cache can go briefly stale.
Write-throughEvery write goes to the cache and the DB at the same timeCache always fresh, but writes are slower (two systems updated synchronously)
Write-backWrite goes to cache first; DB is updated later, asynchronouslyVery fast writes, but risk of data loss if the cache fails before flushing

Eviction Policies & TTLs

A cache is always smaller than the source of truth, so something has to leave when it fills up. LRU (Least Recently Used) evicts whatever hasn't been touched in the longest time — the default in most systems. LFU (Least Frequently Used) evicts whatever is asked for least often overall. A TTL (Time To Live) simply expires an entry after N seconds regardless of usage, bounding how stale data can get.

Cache stampede (thundering herd): when a hot key expires, thousands of requests can simultaneously miss and hammer the database at once. Fixes: stagger TTLs with jitter, use a lock so only one request repopulates the cache while others wait, or proactively refresh hot keys before they expire.

Active Recall

❓ What problem does caching solve, in one sentence? (click to reveal)
It avoids repeating expensive work (usually a database read) by storing the result somewhere faster, cutting both latency and load on the source of truth.

Real-World Engineering

🎬 Netflix

Caches personalized homepage rows (EVCache, built on Memcached) so browsing feels instant across regions.

🐦 Twitter

Caches the social graph and timelines heavily — most reads never touch the primary database.

📦 Amazon

Caches product and pricing pages at multiple layers — CDN, app cache, and database query cache.

🅰️ Airbnb

Uses caching for search results, which are expensive to compute and don't need to be perfectly real-time.

Flashcards (Spaced Repetition)

Q: Why use caching?
A: Reduce latency and database load by avoiding repeated expensive reads.
Q: What's a cache stampede, and one way to prevent it?
A: Many requests miss a hot key at once and overload the DB; prevent it with jittered TTLs or a repopulation lock.
Q: Write-through vs write-back — what's the risk of write-back?
A: Faster writes, but data can be lost if the cache fails before it flushes to the database.

Memory Test — Answer Without Looking Up

  • What's the difference between a cache hit and a cache miss?
  • Name the three core caching patterns and one tradeoff of each.
  • What is a cache stampede and how do you prevent it?
  • What's the difference between LRU and LFU eviction?
  • Why might write-back caching lose data that write-through wouldn't?
If you only remember one thing: caching trades a little bit of staleness for a lot of speed — your job in an interview is to say exactly how much staleness is acceptable.
