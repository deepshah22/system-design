Database Replication
Keeping a Live Copy on Another Machine

Yesterday Database Dave learned to find one row in a billion. Today the ground shifts under him: the single machine holding that billion rows has one power supply, one disk, and one very bad day coming. The fix is obvious — keep a full, live copy on another machine — but the moment a second copy exists, the two can disagree. Everything hard and interesting about replication lives in that disagreement: how fast the copy catches up, what you lose if the original dies mid-write, and who is allowed to accept writes at all.

Visual Memory Anchor

👥📒

Picture this

Two clerks sit in two different buildings, each keeping an identical ledger. Only one of them — the leader — is allowed to write new entries. Every time the leader writes a line, a courier immediately runs a copy of that exact line across the street to the follower, who writes the same line in the same order. Most of the time the two ledgers are the same. But look closely at any single instant: the courier is still in transit, so the follower's book is always a few lines behind. That gap — the courier mid-street — is replication lag, and it is the source of nearly every surprising bug in a replicated system. Picture the courier frozen mid-stride and you've recalled the whole concept.

The Story

Database Dave now runs a fast, well-indexed ledger — and one Tuesday the machine holding it dies. Not slowly: a power supply pops, the disk head parks, and 50 million customer balances are simply gone from the network until someone drives to the data center. For six hours, Dave's business is offline. He swears never again.

So Dave buys a second machine and keeps a live copy of the ledger on it. He decides only the first machine — the leader — accepts new writes; the second — the follower — just receives a stream of every change and applies it in the same order. Now if the leader dies, Dave promotes the follower and stays online. As a bonus, all those read-heavy "what's my balance?" queries can be answered by the follower, so the leader is freed up to handle writes. One copy became fault tolerance and read scaling.

Then a customer complains. She transferred money, saw "success," refreshed the page — and the money was gone. Not really gone: her write went to the leader, but her refresh was answered by the follower, which hadn't received that change yet. The courier was still crossing the street. Dave has just met replication lag and the read-your-own-writes problem, and he's about to discover the deeper fork in the road: should the leader wait for the courier to arrive before telling the customer "success" (safe, but slow), or answer instantly and let the copy catch up (fast, but you can lose that write if the leader dies in the gap)? That single choice — synchronous vs asynchronous — is the heart of today's lesson.

What should stay in your head
Replication keeps live copies of your data on multiple machines, for three reasons: survive failures (availability), serve reads from many copies (scale), and put data near users (latency). The instant you have more than one copy, they can disagree — so every replication design is really an answer to two questions: who can accept writes (one leader, many leaders, or no leader), and does the leader wait for copies before acknowledging a write (synchronous = safe/slow, asynchronous = fast/lossy).

How Replication Actually Works

The replication log is the whole trick

A follower does not "copy the database" over and over. Instead the leader keeps an ordered log of every change it makes — the same write-ahead log (WAL) it already writes for crash recovery — and simply ships that log to each follower. Each follower replays the log entries in exactly the order the leader wrote them, and so arrives at an identical state. Order matters enormously: "set balance = 100" then "add 50" must never be replayed as "add 50" then "set balance = 100." An ordered, replayable log is what makes replicas converge deterministically.

There are a few ways to encode that log, and the choice has real consequences:

MethodWhat shipsGotcha
Statement-basedThe literal SQL (UPDATE … SET x = x+1)Non-deterministic functions (NOW(), RAND(), auto-increment) can produce different results on each replica. Compact but fragile.
Row-based (logical)The concrete rows that changed, with their new valuesDeterministic and replication-safe; the modern default (MySQL row-based binlog, Postgres logical replication). Larger log.
WAL / physicalThe exact byte-level changes to disk pagesFastest and simplest, but ties the follower to the same storage-engine version as the leader.

Synchronous vs asynchronous: the acknowledgement fork

When a client sends a write, the leader must decide when to reply "committed." This is the single most consequential knob in replication:

Asynchronous.The leader writes locally and immediately acks the client, then ships the change to followers "whenever." Fastest possible writes, and one slow follower can't stall the system — but if the leader crashes before the change reaches any follower, that acknowledged write is lost. This is the default in most systems.
Synchronous.The leader waits until follower(s) confirm they have the change, then acks the client. Zero data loss on failover — but the client now waits a full network round-trip, and if a synchronous follower goes down, writes block entirely until it returns or is dropped.
Semi-synchronous (the pragmatic middle).Wait for just one follower to confirm, then ack. That one confirmed copy guarantees the write survives a leader crash, while the other followers stream asynchronously so a single slow node can't freeze you. This is what most production systems actually run.

Notice this is the CAP/PACELC trade from Day 10 wearing work clothes: synchronous replication buys consistency and durability by spending latency (the "Else → Latency" of PACELC), and asynchronous does the reverse.

Failover: promoting a follower when the leader dies

If the leader disappears, one follower must be promoted to leader. This sounds simple and is a minefield: the system must detect the failure (usually a timeout — how long before you declare a leader dead?), choose the most up-to-date follower, and redirect all writes to it. Two classic failures lurk here: losing acknowledged writes (an async follower that was behind gets promoted, and the unreplicated tail is gone), and split-brain — the old leader wasn't actually dead, just unreachable, and now two nodes think they're the leader and both accept writes. Preventing split-brain is exactly why we'll need consensus (Raft, Day 32) to elect leaders safely.

Three Topologies (Who Is Allowed to Write?)

Single-leader

One node accepts writes; all others are read-only followers. Simple, no write conflicts ever, strong ordering. The default for relational databases.

Great for: read-heavy apps, strong consistency. Postgres, MySQL, most SQL DBs.

Multi-leader

Several nodes (often one per region) each accept writes and replicate to the others. Great for multi-datacenter write latency and offline edits — but two leaders can write the same key at once, so you inherit write conflicts that must be resolved.

Great for: multi-region writes, offline-first apps. Needs conflict resolution.

Leaderless (quorum)

No leader at all — the client writes to many replicas and reads from many, using quorums (W + R > N) to guarantee overlap. This is the Dynamo model from Day 10; conflicts are resolved with version vectors and read-repair.

Great for: high availability, always-writable. Cassandra, DynamoDB, Riak.

The multi-leader conflict problem

Single-leader replication has no write conflicts because every write passes through one node that orders them. The moment two leaders can both accept a write to the same row — say a user edits their profile from a laptop in Frankfurt and a phone in Virginia at nearly the same instant — you have two "true" values and must decide which wins. Options range from crude to careful: last-write-wins (pick the higher timestamp — simple, but silently discards the other write), application-level merge (like Google Docs merging edits), or CRDTs (Day 51 — data structures mathematically guaranteed to merge without conflict). There is no free lunch: multi-leader buys write availability and locality at the price of conflict handling you must design for.

The Lag Problem — and How to Live With It

Asynchronous replication means followers are always slightly behind. Usually that's milliseconds and nobody notices. But under load, or when a follower is catching up after a restart, lag can grow to seconds or minutes — and reads from a lagging follower return stale data. Three concrete consistency hazards show up, each with a standard fix:

Read-your-own-writes.You post a comment, refresh, and it's gone — because the refresh hit a follower that doesn't have your write yet. Fix: read your own recent writes from the leader (or a follower known to be caught up), so at minimum you always see your own changes.
Monotonic reads.You refresh twice and the second load shows older data than the first — because the two requests hit followers with different lag. It looks like time ran backwards. Fix: pin a given user to the same replica so they never see the clock go in reverse.
Consistent-prefix reads.You see an answer before the question it replies to, because different partitions replicated at different speeds. Fix: ensure causally related writes are read in order (a real concern once data is partitioned — Day 13).

The engineering point: you don't eliminate lag, you decide what the application can tolerate and route reads accordingly. "Send this read to a follower" is a per-query decision, not a global setting — the account balance goes to the leader, the marketing blog post goes to whatever follower is nearest.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

The misconceptionThe reality
"Replication is a backup."No — a replica faithfully copies your mistakes too. Drop a table and it's dropped on every replica within milliseconds. Backups are point-in-time and independent; replicas are live. You need both.
"Add a read replica and reads are always correct."Async replicas lag. A read from one can return data seconds old. You must decide per-query whether stale is acceptable and route to leader or follower accordingly.
"Synchronous replication has no downside."It adds a full round-trip to every write, and if a synchronous follower dies, writes block until it recovers or is demoted. That's why most systems run semi-sync, not full sync.
"Automatic failover always keeps us safe."Naive failover can lose the unreplicated tail of async writes and cause split-brain (two leaders). Safe failover needs consensus and careful timeout tuning — it's a common source of outages, not just a safety net.
"Multi-leader is just single-leader with more writers."It fundamentally introduces write conflicts that single-leader never has. You must design conflict resolution (LWW, merge, or CRDTs) up front.

Active Recall

❓ Your leader acknowledges a write to the client, then crashes 5 ms later. Under asynchronous replication, what happens to that write — and what one change to the replication mode would have prevented it, at what cost? (click to reveal)
Under async, the leader acked before the change reached any follower, so when a follower is promoted it never had that write — the acknowledged write is permanently lost (the "data-loss window"). Switching to semi-synchronous (wait for at least one follower to confirm before acking) would have prevented it, because at least one surviving copy is guaranteed to hold the write. The cost: every write now pays one extra network round-trip of latency, and if that one synchronous follower is unavailable, writes stall until another takes its place.

Real-World Engineering

🐘 PostgreSQL

Ships streaming WAL replication with a per-standby choice of async or synchronous (synchronous_commit). Teams commonly run one synchronous standby for durability plus several async read replicas for scale, and use tools like Patroni to automate failover safely.

🐬 MySQL

Row-based binlog replication is the backbone of countless read-heavy sites. GitHub famously wrote orchestrator to manage MySQL failover, and semi-synchronous replication is the standard setup to bound data loss without paying full-sync latency.

🪶 Cassandra / DynamoDB

Leaderless: every write goes to N replicas, and tunable quorums (W + R > N) let each query trade consistency for latency. Conflicts resolve via last-write-wins timestamps or version vectors plus read-repair — the Day 10 quorum math, in production.

🌍 Multi-region (Spanner, CockroachDB)

Use consensus (Paxos/Raft) to replicate each shard synchronously across regions, getting strong consistency and survivability — at the cost of cross-region write latency. Spanner leans on tightly synchronized clocks (TrueTime) to make this affordable at global scale.

Flashcards (Spaced Repetition)

Q: What are the three reasons to replicate data?
A: (1) Availability — survive a node/disk/datacenter failure; (2) Read scaling — serve reads from many copies; (3) Latency — keep a copy geographically near users.
Q: State the core trade between synchronous and asynchronous replication.
A: Synchronous waits for follower(s) to confirm before acking — zero data loss on failover but higher write latency and it blocks if a sync follower dies. Asynchronous acks immediately — fastest writes but an acknowledged write can be lost if the leader crashes before it replicates.
Q: Why does single-leader replication have no write conflicts, and what changes with multi-leader?
A: One node orders every write, so there's a single source of truth. Multi-leader lets two nodes accept a write to the same key concurrently, producing conflicts that must be resolved (last-write-wins, application merge, or CRDTs).
Q: What is the read-your-own-writes problem and its fix?
A: A user makes a write to the leader, then a follow-up read hits a lagging follower that doesn't have it yet, so their own change appears to vanish. Fix: route a user's reads of their own recent writes to the leader (or a caught-up replica).
Q: Why is a replica not a backup?
A: A replica copies everything including your mistakes — a dropped table replicates instantly. A backup is an independent point-in-time copy you can restore from. You need both.

Memory Test — Answer Without Looking Up

  • Explain how a follower stays in sync with a leader without re-copying the whole database. What is shipped, and why does order matter?
  • Walk through what happens to an acknowledged write under async vs semi-sync vs full-sync replication when the leader crashes immediately after the ack.
  • Name the three topologies by "who can write," and give one advantage and one cost of each.
  • A user complains their post disappeared after they refreshed. Diagnose the likely cause and give the standard fix.
  • What is split-brain, why does automatic failover risk it, and what mechanism (coming later in the course) prevents it?
  • Your app has one write-heavy dashboard and a mostly-read public feed. Which reads would you route to followers and which to the leader, and why?
If you only remember one thing: replication keeps live copies so you can survive failures and scale reads — but the second copy can always disagree with the first. Every design is an answer to "who may write" (one leader, many, or none) and "does the leader wait for the copy before saying yes" (synchronous = safe and slow, asynchronous = fast and lossy). Master the lag, route each read to the copy whose staleness you can live with, and never mistake a replica for a backup.
