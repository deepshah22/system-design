CAP Theorem & PACELC
The Tradeoff You Cannot Engineer Your Way Out Of

Yesterday, Database Dave's notary desk enforced ACID with total confidence — one desk, one set of rules, no ambiguity. Today his bank opens a second office on another continent, and the single phone line connecting them goes dead. Dave has to make a decision no amount of clever engineering can dodge. This is the one theorem every principal engineer is expected to reason about from memory, correctly, under pressure, for every data store they've ever touched.

Your database runs across two data centers, New York and London, for redundancy. A backhoe three states away slices through the undersea cable connecting them. Both data centers are still alive. Both are still getting user traffic. They just can't talk to each other anymore. A user in New York asks to buy the last concert ticket. Do you sell it to them?

There is no clever answer that avoids the question. If you say yes, you might also sell the same last ticket to someone in London — the two data centers can't compare notes to know it was already sold. If you say no, the New York system is now refusing money it could otherwise take, from a customer who did nothing wrong. Every distributed data store you will ever use has already made this exact choice on your behalf, permanently, in its architecture. Today's job is knowing which choice it made, and why.

Visual Memory Anchor

🌉🪓

Picture this

Two island offices connected by a single rope bridge. Someone cuts the bridge. Each island still has its own staff, its own desk, its own copy of the ledger — but they can no longer shout updates across the gap. Each island now has to decide, alone: keep serving customers using whatever the ledger said last (and risk it being wrong), or close the counter and turn everyone away until the bridge is rebuilt. That decision, made islet by islet, is the entire CAP theorem in one scene.

The Story

Database Dave now runs two notary offices — one in New York, one in London — kept in sync over a single phone line so both offices always agree on every account balance. One morning, the line goes dead. Both offices are open. Both have customers at the counter. Dave, in New York, gets a request to debit an account that London's copy of the ledger says has $50, but Dave has no way to confirm London hasn't already spent that same $50 in the last ten minutes. He has exactly two options, and no third one exists.

Option one: Dave refuses the transaction. "I can't reach London to confirm this balance is still accurate, so I won't touch it." His ledger stays provably correct — but a paying customer standing right in front of him gets turned away for a phone line he doesn't control. That's choosing Consistency over Availability during the partition — CP.

Option two: Dave serves the customer anyway, using his last-known local copy of the balance. The counter stays open, nobody gets turned away — but if London already spent that money in the last ten minutes, Dave's ledger and London's ledger now disagree, and someone has to reconcile that mess once the phone line comes back. That's choosing Availability over Consistency during the partition — AP.

The theorem's third letter, Partition tolerance, isn't really a choice at all — it's the fact that the phone line will go dead sometime, whether Dave likes it or not. Cables get cut, routers fail, data centers lose power. A system that isn't partition-tolerant isn't really distributed; it just hasn't had its bad day yet. CAP isn't "pick any 2 of 3" — it's "partitions are a fact of life, so pick C or A for the moments when one happens."

What should stay in your head
CAP theorem: when a network partition happens (and eventually it will), a distributed system must choose between staying Consistent (refuse requests it can't verify) or staying Available (serve requests using possibly-stale local data) — it cannot do both at the same time, for the same data, during that partition.

CAP In Depth

Consistency — every read sees the latest write

In CAP, Consistency means linearizability: once a write succeeds anywhere, every subsequent read from any node returns that value (or a newer one) — never a stale one. This is a stronger, more specific claim than ACID's "Consistency," which is about respecting constraints, not about which value a read returns across replicas. Conflating the two is the single most common CAP mistake engineers make in interviews.

Availability — every request gets a response

Every request to a non-failing node must receive a response — not an error, not a timeout, not silence. Note what this doesn't promise: it says nothing about whether the response contains the latest data, only that a response comes back at all.

Partition tolerance — the system keeps working despite dropped/delayed messages

A network partition is any situation where messages between nodes are lost or arbitrarily delayed — not just a dramatic backhoe scenario, but also a congested link, a slow router, or a node that's alive but temporarily unreachable. Because partitions are a physical inevitability at any real scale, partition tolerance isn't really optional the way C and A are — it's the precondition that makes the C-vs-A choice show up in the first place. This is why the honest framing is "CP or AP," not "pick any 2 of CAP" — a system that isn't partition-tolerant simply isn't a distributed system operating at scale.

The classic mistake: CAP is not a permanent, system-wide label

Real systems don't pin a single CAP letter to themselves forever. Many systems are tunable per operation (Cassandra lets you choose a consistency level per query), and almost all "AP" systems are still fully consistent 99.9% of the time — CAP only forces a choice during an actual partition. The useful interview move is naming what a system does specifically when a partition happens, not reciting "it's AP" as a trivia fact.

PACELC: The Part CAP Leaves Out

CAP only describes what happens during a partition (P) — but partitions are rare; most traffic, most of the time, hits a perfectly healthy network. PACELC (coined by Daniel Abadi) extends the theorem to cover that far more common case: if a Partition happens, choose Availability or Consistency (as in CAP) — Else (network is fine), choose Latency or Consistency. Even with zero partitions, a system still has to decide whether to wait for every replica to confirm a write (more Consistency, more Latency) or acknowledge as soon as one node has it (less Latency, weaker Consistency).

SystemPACELC classificationWhat it means in practice
DynamoDB / CassandraPA/ELAvailable over consistent during a partition; low-latency over strict consistency the rest of the time — writes ack fast, replicas catch up asynchronously.
MongoDB (default)PC/ECConsistent over available during a partition (no primary, no writes); waits for enough replication to stay strongly consistent even when healthy.
Google SpannerPC/EC (with very high practical availability)Strongly consistent always, using synchronized atomic clocks (TrueTime) to make consistency cheap enough that the latency cost is small — a rare case where excellent engineering narrows, not eliminates, the tradeoff.
MySQL with synchronous replicationPC/ECWaits for replica acknowledgment on every commit — consistent, at a real latency cost even absent any partition.

This is the table a principal engineer actually reaches for — "is this system AP or CP" is an incomplete question; "what does it do during a partition, and what does it trade for latency when healthy" is the complete one.

Active Recall

❓ During an active network partition, what are the only two options a distributed system has — and what does each one cost? (click to reveal)
It can stay Consistent by refusing requests it can't verify against every replica (cost: reduced availability — real users get errors), or it can stay Available by serving requests from local, possibly-stale data (cost: reduced consistency — different nodes may disagree until the partition heals and the data reconciles). There is no third option that gets both during the partition itself.

Feynman Test

Explain to a 10-year-old why two people using the same banking app in different countries might briefly see different account balances, even though nothing is "broken." Write it below, then compare.

"Imagine two friends each keeping a copy of the same notebook, but they can only compare notes by phone. If the phone line goes down for a minute, each friend still has to answer questions using whatever their notebook said last — they can't magically know what the other one just wrote. Once the phone line is back, they compare notes and fix any difference. For that one minute, they might give slightly different answers — not because anyone made a mistake, but because they physically couldn't check with each other in time."

Real-World Engineering

📦 Amazon DynamoDB

Descended directly from the original Dynamo paper — chooses Availability during a partition, using vector clocks and "last write wins" or application-level conflict resolution to reconcile divergent replicas afterward. Built for shopping carts, where "always accept the write" matters more than instant global agreement.

🍃 MongoDB

Chooses Consistency by default — if a primary can't reach a majority of its replica set, it steps down and the partition-isolated minority refuses writes entirely, rather than risk divergent data.

🌐 Google Spanner

Famous for appearing to sidestep CAP by staying strongly consistent with very high availability — achieved through TrueTime, GPS/atomic-clock-synchronized timestamps that let Spanner order transactions globally without waiting on ordinary network round-trips. It's still bound by CAP during a true partition; it just makes the "else" branch of PACELC extremely cheap.

🪶 Apache Cassandra

Doesn't pick one CAP letter globally — the consistency level (ONE, QUORUM, ALL) is chosen per read and per write, letting one team optimize a query for Availability and another optimize a different query on the same cluster for Consistency.

Flashcards (Spaced Repetition)

Q: State the CAP theorem in one sentence.
A: During a network partition, a distributed system must choose between staying Consistent (reject unverifiable requests) or staying Available (serve possibly-stale data) — it cannot fully have both at once.
Q: Why is "pick any 2 of C, A, P" a misleading way to state CAP?
A: Because partitions are a physical inevitability at scale, not an optional design choice — the real decision is only ever CP vs. AP for the moments a partition is actually happening.
Q: What does PACELC add on top of CAP?
A: It covers the far more common non-partition case: even with a healthy network, a system still trades Latency vs. Consistency (the "Else" branch) — e.g. wait for full replica acknowledgment (consistent, slower) or ack after one node (fast, weaker consistency).
Q: Classify DynamoDB and MongoDB by PACELC, in short.
A: DynamoDB is PA/EL (available during a partition, low-latency when healthy). MongoDB (default) is PC/EC (consistent during a partition, consistent when healthy, at a latency cost).

Memory Test — Answer Without Looking Up

  • State the CAP theorem precisely, and explain why "pick 2 of 3" oversimplifies it.
  • Walk through the New York/London partition scenario: what does Dave lose by choosing CP, and what does he risk by choosing AP?
  • What question does PACELC answer that CAP doesn't even ask?
  • Classify DynamoDB, MongoDB, and Spanner by their partition-time and non-partition-time tradeoffs.
  • Why does Cassandra not have a single, fixed CAP classification?
If you only remember one thing: CAP isn't "pick 2 of 3" — partitions are a fact of physics, not a choice, so the real decision every distributed data store makes is CP or AP for the moment a partition hits, and PACELC is the reminder that even on a good day, that same system is still quietly trading latency for consistency on every single write.
