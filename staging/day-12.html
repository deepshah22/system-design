Database Replication
One Copy Is a Single Point of Regret

For two days Database Dave has run two island offices. But he never asked the obvious question: how does London's ledger become a copy of New York's in the first place? Today the answer, and the reason it matters, arrives the hard way — a fire, a dead disk, and a business that either survives or doesn't based entirely on whether a second copy existed. Replication is the difference between "the server died" being a five-minute story and a five-year one.

It's 2 a.m. The single disk holding your company's one and only database — every order, every customer, every dollar owed — clicks twice and dies. There is no second copy. How much of your company still exists at 2:01 a.m.?

If your honest answer is "none of it," you've just discovered why replication isn't an optimization — it's oxygen. But here's the twist that makes it interesting: the moment you keep a second copy, you inherit a brand-new problem you didn't have before. Two copies can disagree. Everything hard about replication — lag, stale reads, split-brain, the entire CAP choice from Day 10 — flows from that single consequence of the thing that saved you.

One ledger burning on a desk — and an identical ledger, safe, on a second island across the water. Every time Dave writes a line in the original, a runner sprints across the bridge and copies that exact line into the twin. When the fire takes the first ledger, the business doesn't die: the twin is right there, a few lines behind at worst. That runner — the thing that copies each write to the second book — is replication. And the handful of lines the runner hadn't copied yet when the fire hit? That gap is every replication problem you'll ever debug.

The Story

Database Dave keeps the master ledger at his New York desk. Every deposit, every withdrawal, every new account gets written there first — that book is the single source of truth. But Dave learned his lesson the night a coffee spill nearly erased a page: one book is one accident away from ruin.

So Dave hires a runner. Every time he writes a line in the master ledger, he hands the runner a copy of that exact line, and the runner sprints it across the rope bridge to the twin ledger on the London island. New York's book is the leader; London's is a follower — a faithful, slightly-delayed replica of everything the leader has ever recorded. This one arrangement quietly solves three problems at once: if New York burns down, London still has (almost) everything (durability); if Dave's desk is unreachable, customers can at least read their balance from London (availability); and Dave can send all the "what's my balance?" questions to London so his own desk is free to keep taking new writes (read scaling).

But the runner is not instant. There is always a moment — a few lines' worth — where New York has recorded something London hasn't received yet. Most of the time, nobody notices. But a customer who deposits money in New York and then immediately checks their balance on London might see the old number, as if their deposit vanished. Nothing is broken. The runner is simply still on the bridge. That gap — the handful of un-copied lines — is called replication lag, and taming it is what the rest of today is about.

What should stay in your head
Replication is keeping the same data on more than one machine so you don't lose it, don't go down with one machine, and can spread reads across many. The single unavoidable cost is that copies drift apart for a moment after every write — so the real design question is never "should I replicate?" but "how do I make the copies agree, and how much lag and disagreement can I tolerate while they catch up?"

The Three Topologies

There are only three fundamental ways to arrange who-copies-to-whom. Every replicated database you'll ever meet is a variation on one of these.

1 · Single-Leader (leader–follower)

One node accepts all writes (the leader). Every other node is a read-only copy (follower) that streams the leader's changes. Simple to reason about, no write conflicts ever — because there's only one place writes happen.

Postgres/MySQL streaming replication, MongoDB replica sets, most managed RDBMS.

2 · Multi-Leader

Several nodes each accept writes and replicate to each other. Great across data centers (each region writes locally, low latency) — but two leaders can edit the same row at once, so you inherit write conflicts that must be resolved.

Multi-region active-active setups, CouchDB, calendar/notes apps that sync offline edits.

3 · Leaderless (quorum)

No leader at all. The client (or a coordinator) writes to several nodes directly and reads from several, using quorums (W + R > N) to guarantee overlap. Highly available, self-healing — but consistency is your job to tune.

Amazon Dynamo, Cassandra, Riak, ScyllaDB.

The quorum rule, in one line

In leaderless systems with N replicas, if every write lands on at least W nodes and every read consults at least R nodes, then setting W + R > N guarantees any read overlaps at least one node that saw the latest write — so a fresh value is always reachable. Classic default: N=3, W=2, R=2. Turn W and R down and you trade consistency for speed and availability; this is the leaderless dial for the exact CAP/PACELC choice from Day 10.

Synchronous vs Asynchronous — the one knob that matters most

Whatever the topology, every replication link is either synchronous (the leader waits for the follower to confirm the write before telling the client "done") or asynchronous (the leader confirms to the client immediately and copies to the follower afterward). This single choice trades data safety against write latency, and it's the thing interviewers push on hardest.

PropertySynchronousAsynchronous
Write latencySlow — bounded by the slowest replica you wait forFast — leader acks alone
Durability on leader crashStrong — an acked write provably exists on ≥1 other nodeWeak — the last few acked writes can be lost forever
Availability of writesFragile — if the synchronous replica is down, writes blockRobust — one dead follower never blocks the leader
Read staleness~zero on the synchronous replicaBounded by replication lag

The production sweet spot most teams choose is semi-synchronous: keep one synchronous follower (so no acked write is ever truly alone) and let the rest be asynchronous (so you don't pay the slowest-of-many tax). It's the "belt, but not ten belts" compromise — and it's exactly what the quorum option in the simulator above approximates.

Replication Lag: Three Bugs Real Users Hit

Asynchronous replication is the default because it's fast and available — which means replication lag is a fact of life, and it produces three specific anomalies that show up as real, confusing bug reports. Knowing their names is a principal-engineer tell.

① Read-your-own-writes violation

A user updates their profile (write → leader), the page reloads and reads from a lagging follower, and their change is gone. They didn't lose data — the follower just hasn't caught up. Fix: route a user's reads to the leader for a short window after they write (or read from the leader for data they can modify).

② Monotonic-reads violation

A user refreshes twice. The first read hits a caught-up follower and shows a new comment; the second read hits a lagging follower and the comment disappears — time appears to move backward. Fix: pin each user to the same replica (e.g. by hashing their user ID) so they never "rewind."

③ Consistent-prefix violation

Across partitions/shards, an answer replicates before the question it replied to, so an observer sees the reply before the thing it responded to — causality appears broken. Fix: keep causally-related writes on the same partition, or track causal ordering explicitly (a preview of vector clocks, Day 35).

Failover & the Split-Brain Nightmare

When the leader dies, someone must promote a follower to become the new leader — that's failover. It sounds simple. It is one of the most dangerous operations in all of distributed systems, and it fails in three named ways:

Lost writes.  With async replication, the dead leader may have acked writes the promoted follower never received. If those writes conflict with new ones (e.g. an auto-increment ID reused), you get silent corruption — GitHub's famous 2012 outage was exactly this.
Split-brain.  The old leader wasn't actually dead — just unreachable. Now two nodes both believe they're the leader and both accept writes. This is the CAP partition (Day 10) at its most destructive: two divergent histories that must later be merged by hand. Prevention needs a majority-quorum "fencing" mechanism so only one side can win.
Bad timeout tuning.  Declare the leader dead too fast and a brief network blip triggers a needless, disruptive failover; too slow and you sit in an outage. There is no universally correct timeout — it's a tradeoff you tune per system.

This is why real systems don't let humans (or a single node) decide "the leader is dead." They use a consensus algorithm — Raft or Paxos (Days 31–32) — so a majority must agree on who leads before any promotion happens. Consensus is, in large part, the machinery that makes safe failover possible. Today plants that seed; those lessons pay it off.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions

The trapThe reality
"Replication is a backup."No. Replication copies every write instantly — including the DROP TABLE you didn't mean. A backup is a point-in-time snapshot you can roll back to; replication faithfully replicates your mistake to all copies. You need both.
"More replicas = more write throughput."Replicas scale reads, not writes. In single-leader, every write still funnels through the one leader — adding followers can even slow writes (more copies to feed). To scale writes you need partitioning (Day 13), not more replicas.
"Sync replication means zero data loss, always."Only for the replicas you synchronously waited on. And it buys that safety with write latency and reduced write availability — waiting on a replica that's down blocks the write entirely.
"Just promote a follower when the leader dies."Naïve failover risks lost writes and split-brain. Safe failover needs quorum-based agreement on who the new leader is.
"Stale reads mean the system is broken."Bounded staleness is the designed price of async replication and read scaling. The fix is choosing the right consistency guarantee (read-your-writes, monotonic reads) per use case — not eliminating lag.

Active Recall

❓ Replication solves three distinct problems at once. Name them — and then name the one problem it does not solve. (click to reveal)
Solves: (1) durability — a second copy survives if one machine dies; (2) availability — you can still serve (at least reads) when one node is down; (3) read scaling — spread read traffic across many copies. Does not solve: write scaling — in single-leader, all writes still funnel through one leader; that needs partitioning (Day 13). Bonus: it's also not a backup — it replicates mistakes just as faithfully as good writes.

Feynman Test

Explain to a 10-year-old why, right after you post a photo, a friend might not see it for a few seconds — even though nothing is broken. Write it below, then compare.

"Imagine the app keeps your photo in a main notebook, and then a helper runs around copying it into a bunch of backup notebooks so it never gets lost. When you post, it goes into the main notebook right away — but your friend's phone happens to be looking at one of the backup notebooks the helper hasn't reached yet. For a few seconds the backup still says 'no photo.' Nobody made a mistake — the helper is just still running. A moment later the copy arrives and your friend sees it. Keeping lots of copies is how the app makes sure your photo never disappears; the tiny delay is the price of having all those copies."

Real-World Engineering

🐘 PostgreSQL / MySQL

Ship single-leader streaming replication out of the box. Teams run one primary plus read-replicas, usually semi-synchronous — one replica confirms each commit (no lonely acked write) while the rest follow asynchronously. Read-heavy services point analytics and dashboards at replicas to spare the primary.

🐙 GitHub (2012 outage)

A botched MySQL failover promoted a stale replica while the old primary was still writing — the textbook split-brain. Two diverging histories had to be reconciled by hand, and the site went down for hours. The lasting lesson the whole industry cites: failover is not "promote a follower," it's a consensus problem.

🪶 Cassandra / DynamoDB

Leaderless quorum replication. You tune consistency per query with N/W/R (e.g. QUORUM reads and writes for W+R>N safety, or ONE for raw speed). Anti-entropy (Merkle trees, Day 37) and read-repair heal divergent replicas in the background.

🌐 Google Spanner

Every shard is a Paxos group: writes replicate synchronously to a majority of replicas across zones before commit, giving strong consistency with survivability. TrueTime clocks keep the latency cost of all that synchronous replication small — replication and consensus fused into one system.

Flashcards (Spaced Repetition)

Q: What three problems does replication solve, and which one does it NOT solve?
A: Solves durability, availability, and read scaling. Does not solve write scaling (single leader is still a write bottleneck) — that needs partitioning. It's also not a backup.
Q: Name the three replication topologies in one line each.
A: Single-leader (one write node, many read followers, no conflicts); multi-leader (many write nodes, low cross-region latency, must resolve write conflicts); leaderless (write/read to quorums, W+R>N for overlap, highly available).
Q: Sync vs async replication — the core tradeoff?
A: Sync waits for the follower before ack → strong durability, slower writes, fragile write availability. Async acks immediately → fast, robust to a dead follower, but the last acked writes can be lost and reads can be stale. Semi-sync (one sync follower) is the common middle ground.
Q: What is the quorum consistency rule and its classic default?
A: With N replicas, if W + R > N (write to W, read from R) every read overlaps a node holding the latest write, so a fresh value is reachable. Classic default: N=3, W=2, R=2.
Q: What is split-brain and how is it prevented?
A: Two nodes both believe they're leader (usually after a partition) and both accept writes, creating divergent histories. Prevented by requiring majority-quorum agreement / fencing so only one side can win — i.e. a consensus algorithm (Raft/Paxos).

Memory Test — Answer Without Looking Up

  • Why is replication not the same thing as a backup? Give a concrete failure that replication does not protect you from.
  • Sketch the three topologies and state the single defining property (and main weakness) of each.
  • A user updates their profile and immediately sees the old value. Name the anomaly and give one fix.
  • Walk through a leader failover and explain the two ways it can corrupt data. How does consensus prevent them?
  • You have N=5 replicas and want strong-ish consistency but decent availability. Pick W and R, and justify the numbers.
If you only remember one thing: replication keeps the same data on many machines so one death can't erase your business — and the instant you have a second copy, the copies can disagree. Every hard part (lag, stale reads, split-brain, the whole CAP choice) is just you managing that disagreement. Sync vs async is the master dial; single-leader / multi-leader / leaderless are the three shapes; and safe failover is a consensus problem, not a button.
